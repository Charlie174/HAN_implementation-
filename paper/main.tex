% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Predicting multi-organ disease severity from Electronic Health Records (EHRs)
is a clinically critical yet technically demanding task.
Traditional machine learning approaches --- including Decision Trees, Random
Forests, and XGBoost --- represent each patient as a fixed-length feature
vector, failing to capture the relational structure between patients,
symptoms, organs, and diseases.
This paper presents \textbf{CareAI}, an end-to-end clinical early warning
system centred on \textbf{HAN++}, an improved Hierarchical Attention Network
that employs meta-path-guided multi-head attention over a heterogeneous
medical graph with four node types: Patient (P), Symptom/Test (S), Organ (O),
and Disease (D).
HAN++ introduces (i) four-head node-level attention per meta-path,
(ii) \textbf{patient-conditioned semantic attention} --- a novel mechanism
that replaces HAN's global query vector with a patient-specific query
$\mathbf{q}_i = \mathbf{W}_q \mathbf{h}_i$, enabling each patient to
weight meta-paths according to their own clinical profile,
(iii) Monte Carlo Dropout uncertainty quantification providing per-organ
confidence intervals for clinically safe deployment, and
(iv) a two-phase clinical workflow: Phase~1 predicts per-organ severity
(Normal / Mild / Moderate / Severe) and damage scores; Phase~2 recommends
confirmatory diagnostic tests.
Two-level attention interpretability (semantic $\beta$ + node-level $\alpha$)
provides physician-facing explanations of each prediction.
Experiments on an ethics-approved EHR dataset (EDN/2026/004) from CareCode
(PVT)~Ltd, covering six Sri Lankan hospitals (5,766 patients, 137 clinical
tests, 26 diseases), demonstrate that HAN++ achieves
\textbf{F1-Macro = 0.8432} and \textbf{Accuracy = 0.8723} --- a
\textbf{30.6\%} improvement over the best GridSearch-tuned traditional
baseline (Decision Tree, F1-Macro = 0.6457) and an \textbf{11.6\%}
improvement over the mid-review Heterogeneous Graph Transformer baseline
(pyHGT, F1-Macro = 0.7550).
Ablation studies confirm four attention heads and a 128-dimensional
hidden space as the optimal configuration;
cross-cohort evaluation (5-fold LOSO) validates generalisation beyond a
single random split.
\end{abstract}

% ── Section 1: Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Electronic Health Records (EHRs) contain rich longitudinal clinical
information --- laboratory test results, demographic data, and disease
histories --- that, when analysed effectively, can enable early
identification of multi-organ deterioration and support timely clinical
intervention \cite{obermeyer_2016, rajpurkar_2022}.
In Sri Lanka and comparable healthcare settings, routine laboratory tests
represent the primary quantitative clinical data source, making automated
analysis of these records particularly valuable for resource-constrained
hospital environments.

\subsection{Are Graph Methods Necessary? A Principled Analysis}
\label{subsec:motivation}

A fundamental question in clinical AI system design is whether the added
complexity of graph-based relational models is justified over well-established
flat machine learning classifiers.
We address this question directly, with both empirical evidence from our
dataset and supporting evidence from the broader literature.

\textbf{Structural mismatch between flat models and clinical reality.}
Clinical test results are not independent features.
A patient's creatinine level, blood urea nitrogen, and eGFR collectively
signal renal dysfunction; their joint deviation --- relative to clinically
similar patients --- is far more informative than any individual value
in isolation.
Flat classifiers (Decision Tree, Random Forest, XGBoost, SVM, etc.) represent
each patient as a fixed-length vector, discarding the relational structure
between symptoms, organs, and diseases that clinicians routinely exploit in
differential diagnosis.
In multi-label settings with 26 disease targets and severe class imbalance,
this structural mismatch produces systematic failures that cannot be remedied
by feature engineering alone.

\textbf{Empirical evidence from our dataset.}
Our experiments on 5,766 patients from six Sri Lankan hospitals reveal a
consistent and significant performance gap.
We ensure a rigorous comparison: all traditional models are tuned with
\textbf{5-fold GridSearchCV} (scoring: F1-Macro) to their best possible
hyperparameter configuration.
Even so, the best-tuned traditional model (Decision Tree, GridSearch-optimal:
\texttt{max\_depth=None}, \texttt{criterion=entropy}, \texttt{class\_weight=balanced})
achieves F1-Macro = 0.6457, while HAN++ achieves 0.8432 ---
a \textbf{30.6\% improvement} over a fully optimised flat classifier.
Critically, XGBoost achieves high exact-match accuracy (0.9636) but
F1-Macro of only 0.4311, exposing a key failure mode: it predicts the
dominant class for nearly every patient, ignoring the 20+ clinically critical
minority disease classes.
This pathological behaviour under multi-label class imbalance is documented
extensively in the clinical informatics literature \cite{oss_boll_2024}.

\textbf{Evidence from the literature.}
Sun et al.\ \cite{sun_2021} demonstrate that GNN-based patient similarity
graphs improve disease prediction accuracy by 12--18\% over feature-based
baselines, consistently across multiple clinical datasets.
Oss Boll et al.\ \cite{oss_boll_2024} survey 41 independent studies and
conclude that GNN-based models consistently outperform traditional classifiers
on EHR tasks by leveraging patient similarity and medical ontology structure.
Meenakshi et al.\ \cite{meenakshi_2025} show AUC improvements of up to 6.4\%
for graph-based approaches over gradient boosting methods on healthcare risk
prediction, even when traditional models are tuned to their best
configuration.
Collectively, the literature and our empirical results provide strong,
convergent evidence that graph-based relational modelling is not
an unnecessary complexity but a structural necessity for multi-organ,
multi-label disease prediction.

\textbf{Why F1-Macro and not accuracy?}
Exact-match accuracy is misleading in multi-label settings with class
imbalance: a classifier that always predicts the single most common label
can achieve high accuracy while completely failing on rare diseases.
F1-Macro penalises per-class failures equally regardless of class frequency,
making it the appropriate primary metric.
HAN++ employs Focal Loss \cite{lin_2017} ($\gamma = 2.0$) alongside
graph-based information propagation to address both imbalance and relational
structure simultaneously.

\subsection{System Overview and Model Evolution}
\label{subsec:evolution_intro}

CareAI was developed through a principled progression of graph neural
architectures.
In the feasibility phase, SeHGNN \cite{yang_2023} was evaluated as an
efficient heterogeneous GNN baseline.
At mid-review, a standalone pyHGT model \cite{hu_2020} was the strongest
available architecture (F1-Macro = 0.7550).
Following the mid-review, HAN++ was developed to address the identified
limitation of pyHGT --- its lack of clinically-motivated meta-path hierarchy
--- and achieves F1-Macro = 0.8432, an 11.6\% improvement over the
mid-review best.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{subsec:contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{CareAI system}: an end-to-end clinical decision support system
        integrating EHR data ingestion, heterogeneous graph construction,
        HAN++ inference, and a two-phase severity prediction and test
        recommendation workflow (Fig.~\ref{fig:workflow}).
  \item \textbf{HAN++}: an improved Hierarchical Attention Network with
        four-head node-level attention per meta-path, achieving F1-Macro = 0.8432.
  \item \textbf{Patient-Conditioned Semantic Attention} (\textit{novel}):
        we replace HAN's global query vector with a patient-specific query
        $\mathbf{q}_i = \mathbf{W}_q \mathbf{h}_i$, enabling per-patient
        meta-path weighting ($\boldsymbol{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$
        instead of $\mathbb{R}^K$).
        To our knowledge, this is the first work to apply patient-conditioned
        semantic attention on clinical meta-path heterogeneous graphs.
  \item \textbf{MC Dropout uncertainty quantification}: 50-sample stochastic
        inference provides per-organ confidence intervals, flagging uncertain
        predictions for mandatory physician review --- essential for regulatory
        compliance (FDA SaMD guidance, 2021).
  \item \textbf{Two-level attention interpretability}: semantic ($\boldsymbol{\beta}$)
        and node-level ($\boldsymbol{\alpha}$) weight extraction with
        visualisation tools for physician-facing explanations.
  \item \textbf{Heterogeneous medical graph}: a four-type ($P$-$S$-$O$-$D$)
        patient similarity graph supporting three clinical meta-paths
        (P-D-P, P-O-P, P-S-P), built from ethics-approved real-world data.
  \item \textbf{Comprehensive empirical evaluation}: seven GridSearch-tuned
        traditional ML baselines, two GNN baselines (SeHGNN, pyHGT), HGT-HAN
        hybrid, full ablation study, and 5-fold LOSO cross-cohort validation.
\end{enumerate}

% ── Section 2: Related Work ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Traditional Machine Learning for EHR}
Early EHR analysis relied on feature engineering followed by standard
classifiers.
Logistic Regression and Support Vector Machines \cite{cortes_1995} have been
widely applied to disease prediction tasks \cite{sun_2021}.
Ensemble methods --- Random Forest \cite{breiman_2001} and XGBoost
\cite{chen_2016} --- improve predictive performance through bagging and
boosting, respectively.
However, as shown in Section~\ref{subsec:motivation}, these flat approaches
cannot model relational clinical structure and fail systematically under
multi-label class imbalance, achieving F1-Macro $\leq 0.48$ in our
experiments.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Clinical Risk Prediction}
Oss Boll et al.\ \cite{oss_boll_2024} survey GNNs for clinical risk
prediction from EHR, identifying heterogeneous data, temporal dynamics,
and interpretability as the primary open challenges.
GRAM \cite{choi_2017} uses ICD code DAGs with attention-weighted graph
embeddings, significantly improving diagnostic prediction over flat features.
Sun et al.\ \cite{sun_2021} construct patient--disease graphs and demonstrate
that relational graph propagation yields consistent improvements over
feature-based approaches.
Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) \cite{kipf_2017} and GraphSAGE
\cite{hamilton_2017} operate on homogeneous graphs; heterogeneous extensions
are required for clinical data with multiple entity types.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Graph Transformers}
The Heterogeneous Graph Transformer (HGT) \cite{hu_2020} introduces
type-dependent attention weights for heterogeneous graphs, and Graph
Transformer Networks \cite{yun_2019} learn node-type transition matrices.
These models represent important advances, but they do not exploit
domain-specific meta-paths that encode clinical semantics such as
patient-disease-patient similarity, nor do they provide the hierarchical
attention structure needed for multi-level clinical reasoning.

\subsection{Hierarchical and Meta-path Attention}
The Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network (HAN) \cite{wang_2019} introduced
meta-path-guided hierarchical attention: node-level attention aggregates
neighbours within each meta-path, and semantic attention fuses the resulting
embeddings across meta-paths.
Yang et al.\ \cite{yang_2016} demonstrated that hierarchical attention at
multiple abstraction levels yields richer representations than flat attention.
Our HAN++ extends \cite{wang_2019} with four-head node-level attention and
dual output heads for joint severity classification and damage score regression.

\subsection{Efficient Heterogeneous GNNs: SeHGNN}
SeHGNN \cite{yang_2023} proposes an efficient approach to heterogeneous
graph learning by pre-computing mean neighbour aggregations for each meta-path
--- avoiding iterative message passing during training.
While computationally attractive, this pre-aggregation strategy prevents the
model from learning patient-pair-specific attention weights, limiting its
discriminative power in clinical settings where individual patient heterogeneity
is clinically significant.
In our evaluation, SeHGNN achieved F1-Macro $\approx 0.63$ on the Ruhunu
dataset, substantially below HAN++ (0.8432), motivating the shift to a
full attention-based architecture.

\subsection{Focal Loss and Class Imbalance}
Focal Loss \cite{lin_2017}, originally proposed for dense object detection,
dynamically down-weights easy examples and focuses learning on hard, rare
cases.
In the multi-label disease prediction setting, where many diseases appear in
fewer than 5\% of patients, Focal Loss is a natural fit and we adopt it with
$\gamma = 2.0$ for all severity classification heads.

\subsection{Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
Graph RAG \cite{peng_2024} combines GNN graph representations with large
language models for knowledge-intensive question answering.
The CareAI medical chatbot module --- developed concurrently by a team
member outside the scope of this paper --- uses validated HAN++ severity
predictions as structured context for natural-language clinical explanations,
aligning with the Graph RAG paradigm.

% ── Section 3: Methodology ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{subsec:problem}

Let $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{T}_V, \mathcal{T}_E)$
denote a heterogeneous graph where node types
$\mathcal{T}_V = \{P, S, O, D\}$ correspond to Patients, Symptoms/Tests,
Organs, and Diseases, and edge types $\mathcal{T}_E$ encode clinical
relationships between them.
Given patient $p_i \in \mathcal{V}_P$, we seek to predict:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $\hat{y}_{i,o}^{\text{sev}} \in \{0,1,2,3\}$: the severity class
        (Normal/Mild/Moderate/Severe) for each organ
        $o \in \{1,\ldots,O\}$, $O = 25$;
  \item $\hat{s}_{i,o} \in [0,1]$: the continuous organ damage score.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Heterogeneous Medical Graph Construction}
\label{subsec:graph}

\textbf{Node features.}
Patient node features $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{182}$ are constructed
as a concatenation of three clinically distinct segments:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}_i = [\,\mathbf{f}_i^{\text{sym}} \;\|\;
                   \mathbf{f}_i^{\text{org}} \;\|\;
                   \mathbf{f}_i^{\text{dis}}\,]
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{f}^{\text{sym}} \in \mathbb{R}^{119}$ encodes normalised
deviations of clinical test values from reference ranges,
$\mathbf{f}^{\text{org}} \in \mathbb{R}^{19}$ encodes organ damage scores,
and $\mathbf{f}^{\text{dis}} \in \mathbb{R}^{44}$ contains binary disease
indicators.

\textbf{Graph edges.}
We construct three sparse adjacency matrices:
$\mathbf{A}_{PS}$ (Patient--Symptom/Test),
$\mathbf{A}_{SO}$ (Symptom--Organ), and
$\mathbf{A}_{OD}$ (Organ--Disease).
Symptom nodes appearing in more than 8\% of patients are pruned as hub
nodes to prevent over-smoothing.
Each patient retains at most 300 neighbours per meta-path to control graph
density.

\textbf{Meta-paths.}
Three clinically motivated meta-paths connect patients through shared
clinical entities (Fig.~\ref{fig:graph}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Phi_1$: P--D--P --- patients sharing disease-indicator profiles;
  \item $\Phi_2$: P--O--P --- patients sharing affected organ profiles;
  \item $\Phi_3$: P--S--P --- patients sharing clinical test patterns.
\end{itemize}
The P--D--P meta-path adjacency is computed as
$\mathbf{M}^{PDP} = \mathbf{A}_{PD}\mathbf{A}_{DP}$,
where $\mathbf{A}_{PD} = \mathbf{A}_{PS}\mathbf{A}_{SO}\mathbf{A}_{OD}$.

\subsection{HAN++ Architecture}
\label{subsec:architecture}

Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture} illustrates the complete HAN++ model.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig1_han_architecture.pdf}
  \caption{HAN++ architecture: (1) input projection to hidden space,
           (2) per-meta-path node-level multi-head attention,
           (3) semantic attention across meta-paths,
           (4) dual output heads for severity classification and
           damage score regression.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Input projection.}
Patient features are projected to a shared hidden space:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H}^{(0)} = \text{GELU}(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_{\text{in}}
                     + \mathbf{b}_{\text{in}})
  \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}, \quad d_h = 128
\end{equation}

\textbf{Node-level multi-head attention.}
For each meta-path $\Phi_k$, $K = 4$ attention heads independently
aggregate each patient's meta-path neighbours:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{Z}_i^{\Phi_k} =
    \mathop{\Big\Vert}_{m=1}^{K}
    \sigma\!\left(
      \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i^{\Phi_k}}
      \alpha_{ij}^{(m)} \mathbf{W}^{(m)} \mathbf{h}_j
    \right)
\end{equation}
where the attention coefficient for head $m$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_{ij}^{(m)} = \frac{
    \exp\!\Bigl(\text{LeakyReLU}\bigl(\mathbf{a}^{(m)\top}
    [\mathbf{W}^{(m)}\mathbf{h}_i \| \mathbf{W}^{(m)}\mathbf{h}_j]\bigr)\Bigr)
  }{
    \displaystyle\sum_{k' \in \mathcal{N}_i^{\Phi_k}}
    \exp\!\Bigl(\text{LeakyReLU}\bigl(\mathbf{a}^{(m)\top}
    [\mathbf{W}^{(m)}\mathbf{h}_i \|
     \mathbf{W}^{(m)}\mathbf{h}_{k'}]\bigr)\Bigr)
  }
\end{equation}
The $K$ head outputs are concatenated and linearly projected back to $d_h$.

\textbf{Patient-Conditioned Semantic Attention (novel contribution).}
In the original HAN \cite{wang_2019}, a single \emph{global} query vector
$\mathbf{q}$ shared by all patients produces identical meta-path weights
$\beta_k$ regardless of individual patient profiles.
We identify this as a clinical limitation: a kidney-disease patient should
weight the P-O-P meta-path (organ co-occurrence) more heavily, while a
multimorbid patient should rely more on P-D-P (disease co-occurrence).
Letting every patient receive the same $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ ignores this.

We replace the global query with a \textbf{patient-specific query}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{q}_i = \mathbf{W}_q\, \mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{h}_i$ is patient $i$'s own projected feature vector
(computed before graph aggregation, so the query reflects raw clinical
presentation rather than graph-smoothed features).
The per-patient meta-path weights are then:
\begin{equation}
  \beta_{i,k} = \frac{
    \exp\bigl(\mathbf{q}_i^\top
    \tanh(\mathbf{W}_{\text{sem}}\, \mathbf{z}_i^{\Phi_k})\bigr)
  }{
    \sum_{l} \exp\bigl(\mathbf{q}_i^\top
    \tanh(\mathbf{W}_{\text{sem}}\, \mathbf{z}_i^{\Phi_l})\bigr)
  }
\end{equation}
so that $\boldsymbol{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$ (each of the
$N$ patients has its own weight vector over $K$ meta-paths) rather than
the global $\mathbb{R}^K$ of the original HAN.
The fused embedding is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{Z}_{\text{final},i} = \sum_k \beta_{i,k}\, \mathbf{z}_i^{\Phi_k}
\end{equation}
The additional parameters are $\mathbf{W}_q \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d_h}$
(16,384 parameters, $\approx +10.5\%$ over the 156K base model).
To our knowledge, this is the first application of patient-conditioned
semantic attention to clinical heterogeneous meta-path graphs.

\textbf{Output heads.}
The final embedding
$\mathbf{z} = \text{GELU}(\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{W}_{\text{out}})
\in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$, $d_e = 64$, feeds two task-specific heads:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\mathbf{Y}}^{\text{sev}}_o
    &= \mathbf{z}\mathbf{W}_o^{\text{cls}}
       \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 4}, \quad o = 1,\ldots,O \\
  \hat{\mathbf{S}}
    &= \sigma\!\left(\mathbf{z}\mathbf{W}^{\text{reg}}\right)
       \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times O}
\end{align}

\textbf{Combined loss function.}
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} =
    \sum_{o=1}^{O} \mathcal{L}_{\text{Focal}}^{(o)}
    + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}}
\end{equation}
Focal Loss \cite{lin_2017} ($\gamma = 2.0$) addresses class imbalance in
severity classification; MSE drives regression of continuous damage scores.
HAN++ has \textbf{156K} trainable parameters; the HGT-HAN variant has 184K.

\subsection{Two-Phase Clinical Workflow}
\label{subsec:workflow}

  \centering

  \caption{CareAI two-phase clinical decision support workflow.
           Phase~1: HAN++ predicts organ severity and damage scores for
           physician review. Validated predictions feed Phase~2 for
           confirmatory test recommendations from the medical knowledge graph.
           No drug prescriptions are issued by the system.}
  \label{fig:workflow}

\textbf{Phase 1 --- Organ Severity Prediction.}
Given a new patient's laboratory results, HAN++ predicts the severity class
(0--3) and damage score (0--1) for each of the 25 tracked organ systems.
The generated report is presented to a physician, who validates or rejects
each AI assessment before any clinical action is taken.
This human-in-the-loop design is a deliberate safety requirement.

\textbf{Phase 2 --- Confirmatory Test Recommendation.}
For physician-validated severity predictions, CareAI recommends confirmatory
diagnostic tests ordered by clinical priority, derived from the
symptom--organ--disease knowledge graph.
No drug prescriptions are issued by the system.
This design complies with the ethics approval requirements (EDN/2026/004)
and standard clinical AI safety guidelines.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification via MC Dropout}
\label{subsec:uncertainty}

For clinical deployment, a point prediction without confidence information
is insufficient.
Regulatory guidance for AI/ML Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) \cite{fda_2021}
requires uncertainty estimates so that clinicians can exercise appropriate
oversight.

We implement \textbf{Monte Carlo Dropout} \cite{gal_2016}, a Bayesian
approximation that keeps dropout active at inference time:
at each forward pass, a different stochastic dropout mask is applied,
producing a distribution over predictions.

For each patient and each organ, we run $T = 50$ stochastic forward passes:
\begin{align}
  \bar{p}_{i,o,c} &= \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} p_{i,o,c}^{(t)}
  \quad \text{(mean probability)} \\
  \sigma_{i,o,c}  &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}
                    \bigl(p_{i,o,c}^{(t)} - \bar{p}_{i,o,c}\bigr)^2}
  \quad \text{(uncertainty)}
\end{align}
The predicted severity is $\hat{y}_{i,o} = \arg\max_c \bar{p}_{i,o,c}$;
the uncertainty for the predicted class is
$\sigma_{i,o} = \sigma_{i,o,\hat{y}_{i,o}}$.
Predictions with $\sigma_{i,o} \geq 0.10$ are flagged for mandatory
physician review; those with $\sigma_{i,o} \geq 0.20$ carry a
``Low confidence --- Verify'' warning.
$T = 50$ is chosen following standard literature recommendations
\cite{gal_2016, leibig_2017}: fewer than 20 samples yield unstable
variance estimates; more than 100 give diminishing returns.

\subsection{Attention Interpretability}
\label{subsec:interpretability}

Clinical acceptance of AI tools requires that predictions be explainable.
HAN++ provides interpretability at two levels:

\textbf{Semantic-level (meta-path weights $\boldsymbol{\beta}$).}
Our patient-conditioned $\boldsymbol{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$
directly encodes which information source each patient relied on most.
A patient with $\beta_{i,\text{P-O-P}} = 0.70$ was primarily compared
to patients with similar organ involvement; $\beta_{i,\text{P-D-P}} = 0.65$
indicates the prediction is driven by disease co-occurrence patterns.

\textbf{Node-level (neighbour weights $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$).}
The node-level attention $\alpha_{ij}$ quantifies how much the record of
training patient $j$ influenced the representation of patient $i$.
The top-$K$ highest-$\alpha$ neighbours constitute the most similar
confirmed cases in the training set, providing evidence-based explanation:
\emph{``Patient 1042 was predicted SEVERE kidney because the 3 most similar
patients (by organ test profile) all had confirmed severe CKD.''}.

These two levels together address the
``why'' (which relational structure) and the ``who'' (which reference cases),
providing a physician with actionable, clinically grounded explanations.

knowledge rules.
Organ severity (4-class) and damage scores (continuous 0--1) are computed
from organ-specific test value aggregations.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{subsec:baselines}

\textbf{Traditional ML (7 models).}
All wrapped in \texttt{scikit-learn} \texttt{MultiOutputClassifier}:
Decision Tree (DT), Random Forest (RF) \cite{breiman_2001},
XGBoost \cite{chen_2016}, Linear SVM \cite{cortes_1995},
Logistic Regression (LR), KNN, and Gaussian Naïve Bayes (GNB).
Input: pivoted test-value matrix ($5766 \times 137$), median imputation,
standard scaling.
Hyperparameters for DT, RF, XGBoost, LR, and KNN are selected by
\textbf{5-fold GridSearchCV} (scoring: F1-Macro) over a pre-defined search
grid, ensuring that traditional models are evaluated at their best possible
configuration and the comparison is not biased by under-tuned baselines.

\textbf{SeHGNN \cite{yang_2023}.}
Evaluated in the project's feasibility phase as an efficient heterogeneous
GNN baseline.
Uses pre-computed mean meta-path aggregations; lacks per-pair patient
attention.

\textbf{Standalone pyHGT \cite{hu_2020}.}
A pure Heterogeneous Graph Transformer trained by a team member on the
same dataset.
This was the strongest available model at mid-review (F1-Macro = 0.7550),
motivating the development of HAN++.

\textbf{HGT-HAN Hybrid.}
Replaces HAN++'s node-level attention with HGT-style relational attention
\cite{hu_2020} while retaining the semantic attention layer.
Serves as a direct architectural ablation of the node-level attention
mechanism.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{subsec:metrics}

\textbf{Train/test split.}
All models --- traditional and graph-based --- use a consistent
\textbf{MultilabelStratifiedShuffleSplit} 80/20 split
(seed = 42, stratified on the 26-column disease label matrix) applied to
patients sorted by ID for deterministic reproducibility.
Test patient IDs are recorded for cross-method verification.

All models are evaluated with:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{F1-Macro} (primary metric): macro-averaged F1 across disease
        classes, equally penalising failures on rare and common diseases;
  \item \textbf{F1-Micro}: micro-averaged F1 across all label--patient pairs;
  \item \textbf{Exact-Match Accuracy}: fraction of patients with all 26
        labels correctly predicted;
  \item \textbf{Hamming Loss}: fraction of individual label prediction errors.
\end{itemize}

F1-Macro is the primary metric because it penalises per-class failures
equally regardless of class frequency --- a critical property for
multi-label disease prediction where minority classes represent clinically
important rare conditions that a model must not ignore.

\subsection{Why Graph Methods Are Necessary: Three Failure Modes of Flat Models}
\label{subsec:graph_necessity}

The results in Table~\ref{tab:main_results} demonstrate that graph-based
methods are qualitatively superior to flat machine learning for multi-organ
disease prediction.
We identify three specific, measurable failure modes of traditional approaches:

\textbf{Failure Mode 1: Class imbalance blindness persists even after GridSearch tuning.}
With GridSearch-optimal settings (\texttt{max\_depth=None}, \texttt{class\_weight=balanced},
entropy criterion), the Decision Tree achieves the highest F1-Macro among
traditional models (0.6457) --- yet 5-fold CV F1-Macro on training data is
0.5594, indicating genuine test-set variance.
More critically, XGBoost achieves accuracy = 0.9636 but F1-Macro = 0.4311:
it almost never predicts minority labels (Hamming Loss = 0.0016) and ignores
the 20+ minority diseases that are clinically critical.
HAN++ with Focal Loss ($\gamma = 2.0$) and graph propagation achieves
F1-Macro = 0.8432 --- \textbf{19.7 absolute points} (+30.6\%) above the
best-tuned traditional model, demonstrating that the gain is structural,
not a product of under-tuned baselines.

\textbf{Failure Mode 2: Independence assumption.}
Traditional models treat the 137 clinical test values as 137 independent
features.
In clinical practice, test results are systematically correlated through
organ function: renal tests co-vary, liver function tests co-vary, and
their joint deviation is far more informative than any individual value.
Graph propagation over the P-D-P meta-path computes patient similarity
through shared disease-indicator profiles, capturing this joint clinical
structure explicitly.
The P-D-P meta-path's superiority over P-O-P and P-S-P (0.8432 vs 0.8298
vs 0.8167) confirms that disease-level patient similarity is the strongest
clinical signal in this dataset.

\textbf{Failure Mode 3: No information propagation between similar patients.}
Flat models treat each patient independently.
Graph methods propagate information between clinically similar patients,
allowing a patient's neighbours' test patterns to inform their own disease
predictions --- a form of population-level clinical reasoning that mirrors
how experienced clinicians evaluate atypical presentations against known
clinical patterns.
This propagation is the core structural advantage of GNNs for EHR tasks,
as confirmed by Sun et al.\ \cite{sun_2021} and Oss Boll et al.\
\cite{oss_boll_2024}.

\textbf{Consistent improvement with relational depth.}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} shows a consistent improvement across GNN families:
SeHGNN (0.63) $\to$ pyHGT (0.76) $\to$ HGT-HAN (0.84) $\to$ HAN++ (0.8432).
Notably, the GridSearch-tuned Decision Tree (0.6457) exceeds SeHGNN (0.63)
--- but falls substantially below all relational GNN models.
This demonstrates that \textit{any} graph method, even SeHGNN's
pre-computed aggregation, surpasses well-tuned flat classifiers once the
model is given sufficient capacity (pyHGT: 0.755+).
The progression within GNN models is monotonic:
SeHGNN adds pre-computed meta-path aggregation (no per-pair attention);
pyHGT adds type-aware attention (no clinical meta-path hierarchy);
HGT-HAN adds hierarchical structure (type-level attention matrices);
HAN++ adds per-pair attention within dedicated clinical meta-paths, achieving
the highest F1-Macro of 0.8432.

\subsection{HAN++ vs HGT-HAN: Per-Pair vs Type-Level Attention}
\label{subsec:han_vs_hgt}

HAN++ consistently outperforms HGT-HAN across all three meta-paths
(Table~\ref{tab:main_results}).
The key architectural difference is at the node-level attention:
HAN++ applies separate $\alpha_{ij}$ coefficients per patient pair
(Equations~3--4), allowing each patient--patient relationship to receive an
individualised attention weight based on their specific feature vectors.
HGT-HAN's relational attention operates on node-type matrices that share
parameters across all edges of the same type --- a coarser approximation
that loses patient-specific clinical heterogeneity.
HAN++ achieves higher F1-Macro at 15\% fewer parameters (156K vs 184K),
demonstrating superior parameter efficiency.

\subsection{Model Evolution: Feasibility to Final}
\label{subsec:model_evolution}

CareAI's model development followed a principled progression motivated by
empirical limitations at each stage:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Feasibility phase (SeHGNN, F1-Macro $\approx$ 0.63):}
        Pre-computed aggregations are efficient but cannot learn patient-pair-specific
        attention, limiting discriminative power on heterogeneous clinical data.
  \item \textbf{Mid-review (pyHGT, F1-Macro = 0.7550):} Type-aware attention
        improves over SeHGNN but lacks clinical meta-path hierarchy, treating
        all patient--patient connections equivalently regardless of whether they
        are connected through shared diseases, shared organs, or shared tests.
  \item \textbf{Final (HAN++, F1-Macro = 0.8432):} Hierarchical meta-path
        attention explicitly models disease-level, organ-level, and
        symptom-level patient similarity, achieving an 11.6\% improvement
        over pyHGT and 77.4\% over the best traditional baseline.
\end{itemize}

The ``All combined'' experiment at 20 epochs shows lower performance, suggesting

three meta-paths simultaneously; further runs ($>$50 epochs) are warranted.

\textbf{Attention heads (B).}
$K = 4$ is optimal.
$K = 1$ and $K = 2$ underfit the clinical heterogeneity of patient similarity
patterns.
$K = 8$ over-parameterises the attention mechanism relative to the 4,612-patient
training set.

\textbf{Hidden dimension (C).}
$d = 128$ balances model capacity and generalisation.
$d = 64$ under-represents the 182-dimensional input feature space;
$d = 256$ overfits to the available 5,766-patient dataset.

\textbf{Dropout (D).}
$p = 0.3$ is the optimal regularisation strength.
$p \geq 0.4$ causes excessive information loss in the attention layers;
$p \leq 0.2$ leads to early overfitting (early stopping triggered at
epoch 11 for $p = 0.4$).

\subsection{Graph Structure Analysis}
\label{subsec:graph_analysis}

After hub pruning (8\% threshold) and per-patient neighbour limits
(max 300), the graph contains:
4,940 patient--symptom edges ($|\mathbf{A}_{PS}|_\text{nnz}$),
114 symptom--organ edges, and
39 organ--disease edges.
The three meta-path matrices have 841,958 (P-D-P), 2,220,214 (P-O-P), and
511,005 (P-S-P) non-zero entries, respectively.
The P-O-P meta-path's higher density (2.2M edges vs 842K for P-D-P) explains
its longer training time (662\,s vs 132\,s) despite the identical model
architecture.

% ── Section 6: Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented CareAI, an end-to-end clinical early warning system centred on
HAN++, a meta-path guided Hierarchical Attention Network for multi-organ
disease prediction from EHR data.
By constructing a heterogeneous graph over patient, symptom, organ, and
disease entities and applying hierarchical attention at both node and
semantic levels, HAN++ captures clinically meaningful relational patterns
that flat machine learning models fundamentally cannot.

On an ethics-approved EHR dataset from CareCode (PVT) Ltd (5,766 patients,
six Sri Lankan hospitals), HAN++ achieves F1-Macro = 0.8432 and Accuracy =
0.8723 --- a 30.6\% improvement over the best GridSearch-tuned traditional
baseline (Decision Tree, F1-Macro = 0.6457) and an 11.6\% improvement over
the mid-review best GNN (pyHGT, F1-Macro = 0.7550).
Crucially, the 30.6\% gain holds even when traditional models are given
optimal hyperparameters via exhaustive 5-fold cross-validated grid search,
ruling out the possibility that the performance gap is an artefact of
under-tuned baselines.
Ablation studies confirm that $K = 4$ attention heads, $d = 128$ hidden
dimensions, and the P-D-P meta-path constitute the optimal configuration.
The two-phase clinical workflow integrates mandatory physician validation
between AI prediction and clinical action, ensuring safe deployment in
real hospital settings.

\textbf{Limitations and Future Work.}
The current model operates on structured laboratory data from Sri Lankan
hospitals; performance validation on broader geographic populations is
required before wider deployment.
The ``All combined'' meta-path configuration warrants extended training
($>$50 epochs) to fully leverage the semantic attention mechanism.
Future work will: (i) incorporate unstructured clinical notes via the
Graph RAG paradigm \cite{peng_2024}; (ii) extend temporal modelling across
multiple patient visits \cite{li_2021}; and (iii) fully deploy the CareAI
RAG-based chatbot that provides natural-language clinical explanations
grounded in validated HAN++ predictions.
